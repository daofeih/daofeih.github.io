Recently, I learned the concept of [**freewriting**](https://writingprocess.mit.edu/process/step-1-generate-ideas/instructions/freewriting/). It's a writing strategy developed by [Peter Elbow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Elbow) in 1973. It encourages you to write immediately and continuously; no need to worry about whether things you write down are right or wrong; writing will help you to think.

I realized that I can apply this technique in my note-taking process while reading a book. The main step is to move the "spot" from the book to my brain. A traditional note-taking way is to take quotes and to write some reflections or comments on them, or to write a summary in our own words. Alternatively, the freewriting method requires me *to take notes from my brain*; I can write down anything that sparks in my brain while reading the book; I don't need to worry about their contents, forms, styles, or anything else.

Here are some pros of this method:

1. *It promotes deeper thinking.*  
   Freewriting pushes you to process information actively. Instead of copying or paraphrasing the book, you're constantly asking, *"What does this mean to me?"* or *"Where does this lead my mind?"*
2. *It captures genuine insight.*  
   Because you're not censoring or editing as you go, you often capture original, spontaneous thoughts—ideas you might never articulate through conventional note-taking. It becomes a record of discovery, not just understanding.
3. *It boosts creativity.*  
   Freewriting unlocks associative thinking. It encourages digressions, questions, analogies, and personal connections, which can lead to unexpected insights or creative interpretations of what you're reading.
4. *It overcomes perfectionism.*  
   Traditional notes often come with pressure to be clear, accurate, or polished. Freewriting removes that pressure, helping you build a more fluid and confident relationship with both writing and learning.
5. *It strengthens writing fluency.*  
   As you make freewriting a habit, you’ll become faster and more fluent in turning thoughts into language—helpful not just for notes, but for essays, articles, and creative writing too.
6. *It reveals your thought process.*  
   You begin to see *how* you think, not just *what* you think. Patterns in your associations, questions you return to, or emotional responses to certain ideas can all be revealed through freewriting.
7. *It makes reading more engaging.*  
   Instead of passively absorbing content, you’re in an ongoing dialogue with the book. Reading becomes more interactive and alive.

Last but not least, I can still take quotes when necessary, but I should keep them minimum and be concise; just imagine them as accents or hooks.
