Thus, in principle, I can write about anything on this blog. However, I believe that most posts will be reading notes. I love reading and taking notes on everything I read. I prefer [Markdown](https://www.markdownguide.org/) for its simplicity and long-term readability—it’s a lightweight, open format that will still make sense a hundred years from now. Since I want to publicize these notes, publishing them as blog posts using a static site generator like [Jekyll](https://jekyllrb.com/) feels like a natural choice. That’s the original purpose of this blog.

## My Note-Taking Method

My note-taking method follows three steps: **reading**, **thinking**, and **writing from memory**.

Whenever I read, I proceed in small, deliberate chunks—a few pages or a coherent section. Once I finish that section, I close the book and ask myself: *What did I just read?*

I then write from memory. This note may include:

- A **summary** of the ideas in my own words.
- A **reflection** or **critique**: Do I agree? Does it remind me of something else?
- A **connection**: How does this idea relate to other books or experiences?

Only after I’ve written the note do I go back to the text. I verify whether my understanding was accurate, revise my note if necessary, and—only if I find something truly striking—I may jot down a short excerpt. But I avoid long quotations: they cost too much time and rarely help me understand better.

## Why I Prefer This Method

1. It forces me to understand, not just copy.

   When I paraphrase from memory, I have no choice but to process the ideas. This makes passive reading nearly impossible.

2. It strengthens long-term memory.

   The act of retrieving what I just read—known in cognitive science as [*active recall*](https://www.bcu.ac.uk/exams-and-revision/best-ways-to-revise/active-recall)—is one of the most effective ways to remember information.

3. It reveals misunderstanding.

   When I compare my memory-based notes with the actual text, I often find gaps, distortions, or missed nuances. This gives me a chance to correct them immediately.

4. It invites reflection.

   Because I’m not stuck copying sentences, I can focus more on what the text means to me. I naturally begin to think critically, form opinions, and make connections.

## Limitations and Trade-offs

Of course, this method isn’t perfect.

- It takes more mental effort.

  You can’t just cruise through pages. Every section requires focus, reflection, and synthesis.

- It may sacrifice precision.

  When paraphrasing complex arguments (especially in philosophy or science), important terms or formulations might get lost. That’s why verifying against the original text is essential.

- It doesn’t work well for reference-heavy reading.

  In technical or academic reading, where exact wording or data is key, excerpts may still be necessary.
