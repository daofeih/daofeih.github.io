For a long time, I searched for the “ideal” way to take reading notes. There’s no shortage of popular methods: the classic “excerpt + commentary” format, knowledge management systems like [Zettelkasten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zettelkasten), or efficiency-driven frameworks with rigid structures. At first, I earnestly tried them all, hoping to find a rhythm of reading that worked for me within a “correct” system.

But gradually, I realized those methods weren’t for me. They may suit readers with clear goals and structured habits, but they couldn't accommodate the leaps, curiosities, and occasional aimlessness of my reading experience. Instead of reading to think, I felt like I was reading just to take notes.

So I gave up.

I abandoned the pursuit of any so-called note-taking method. I decided to let my reading notes be completely unstructured—that’s the most honest reflection of how I read right now.

I only record what interests me, and skip what doesn’t. To me, a book isn’t something that needs to be thoroughly “covered.” It’s a medium that sparks thought. What matters isn’t how much I record, but whether something truly resonates with me.

Sometimes I jot down direct excerpts, preserving the author’s language. Sometimes I summarize, trying to clarify their argument. Sometimes I critique or push back, engaging in debate. Sometimes I reflect and draw connections, using the text as a mirror for my own experience. There’s no fixed format, no set procedure—just whatever my mind demands at that moment.

And in this very state of “disorder,” I began to feel that my notes were no longer a passive record of what I read, but an extension of my thinking. Excerpts are a dialogue with the author. Summaries are tests of my understanding. Critiques are ways of developing my position. Associations and reflections are bridges from the book to the broader world.

By abandoning rigid structures, I’ve begun to build my own.

Perhaps this is my ideal form of note-taking—not perfect, not formulaic, but entirely in service of my thoughts. What I write down is not the book itself, but what happened between me and the book.
