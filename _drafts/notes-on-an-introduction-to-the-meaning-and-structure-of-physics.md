## On the Problem of Motion

### 1. The Tower of Pisa

Civilization is very fragile. After the decline of the once glorious Greek civilization, only a few of its original documents were left in Europe; there were a large number of translations of Greek documents in the Arab world, which were translated into Latin and then flowed back to Europe, which led to the Renaissance. So today's Europe understands Greek civilization through translations that have been passed on several times; to what extent can they get close to the original meaning of Greek documents? It is impossible to evaluate. This also made me start to reflect on one of my paranoias: my English is only half-baked, but I try to read the original English works rather than the translations. Maybe I can get more from reading the translations than from reading the original works. There is no shame in reading translations.

#### Aristotle on the Motion of Bodies

> The earth was the center of Aristotle’s universe and physics. Heavy things fell toward it and light things rose.

What about stars? They don't fall or rise; they rotate around the Earth. What is weight?

> In addition to material bodies—whose natural motions were down or up—he introduced the idea of celestial material (the stuff out of which the stars and planets were made) whose natural motion was in a circle about the center of the universe.

In fact, this description fits on the observation.

#### Criticism

> In the fourteenth century [Nicole Oresme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicole_Oresme), a member of a Parisian school, disputed Aristotle s argument for the earth’s uniqueness. He proposed that the motion of the stars cannot be deduced from their apparent motion viewed from the earth because these two motions are relative; the stars might be at rest while the earth moves just as easily as the earth could be at rest while the stars move—either would produce the same effect.

> Aristotle’s theory of projectile motion came under continual criticism. It was difficult to understand why a projectile continued moving upward (not its natural motion) once the impelling force was removed.

We have reason to believe that Aristotle wouldn't insist that his theory is undeniable since he once said:

> Plato is dear to me, but dearer still is the truth.
