> The Turtle program began as a bet between [Richard Dennis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Dennis) and Bill Eckhardt over whether trading can be taught. Richard was willing to stake his own money on the idea that trading can be taught.

[Curtis Faith](https://www.turtletrader.com/curtis-faith/) is the most successful Turtle. His book *Way of the Turtle: The Secret Methods that Turned Ordinary People into Legendary Traders* (2007) is, as [Van Tharp](https://x.com/vantharp) quotes it, "one of the five best trading books ever written". These are his reasons:

1. "it paints a very clear picture of what is necessary for trading success."
2. "it probably has the most lucid description of how some of the principles of behavioral finance apply to and influence trading that I've ever read."
3. "its emphasis on game theory and the way it uses game theory to explain how a trader should think."

## 1. Risk Junkies

> Investors are people who buy things for the long haul with the idea that over a considerable period—many years—their investments will appreciate in value.

> Traders care about price; essentially they buy and sell *risk*.

> In his informative and engaging book [*Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk*](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/128429.Against_the_Gods), Peter Bernstein discusses how markets developed to allow the transfer of risk from one party to another. This is indeed the reason financial markets were created and a function they continue to serve.

> The act of buying or selling futures contracts to offset business risks caused by price changes in raw materials or fluctuations in foreign currency exchange rates is known as *hedging*.

### Traders Trade Risk

> Many traders—perhaps most of them—are very short-term operators who trade in what is known as *liquidity risk*. This refers to the risk that a trader will not be able to buy or sell: There is no buyer when you want to sell an asset or no seller when you want to buy an asset.

If you want to trade, you will face two prices: the bid and ask prices. Usually, the ask price is higher than the bid price. If you want to buy the asset, you need to buy at the ask price; If you're going to sell the asset, you need to sell at the bid price. The difference between the ask and bid prices is called the *spread*, which can measure liquidity. If you buy the asset and sell it immediately, the spread is the money you lose. The spread is the cost of trading. Thus the higher the spread, the higher the cost of trading, so the lower the liquidity.

> Traders who trade liquidity risk often are referred to as *scalpers* or *market makers*. They make their money off the spread.

> *Price risk* refers to the possibility that prices will move significantly up or down.

> Traders who jump on price risk are known as *speculators* or *position traders*.
