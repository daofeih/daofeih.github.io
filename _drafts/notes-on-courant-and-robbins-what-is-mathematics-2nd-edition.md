## Preface to the Second Edition by Ian Stewart

> *What Is Mathematics?* is one of the great classics, a sparkling collection of mathematical gems, one of whose aims was to counter the idea that "mathematics is nothing but a system of conclusions drawn from definitions and postulates that must be consistent but otherwise may be created by the free will of the mathematician." In short, it wanted to put the meaning back into mathematics.

Thus, mathematics is not a pure "concept game;" there is a bond between mathematics and reality. This doesn't mean every mathematical object has a corresponding realistic object; for example, there are no real objects corresponding to numbers and points. The laws governing these mathematical objects make mathematics meaningful; they make mathematics a *model* of reality.

> It doesn't matter what mathematical things *are*: it's what they *do* that counts.

[Timothy Gowers](https://www.dpmms.cam.ac.uk/~wtg10/) had expressed the same idea in his book [*Mathematics: A Very Short Introduction*](https://academic.oup.com/book/473).

Ian Stewart added a new chapter, "Recent Developments," in the 2nd edition, but he didn't introduce some new topics, such as chaos and broken symmetry. We can find these topics in his book [*From Here to Infinity*](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/from-here-to-infinity-9780192832023).

## What Is Mathematics?

To develop calculus, mathematicians in the 17th century gave up rigor; they hadn't built a deductive system like Euclid did in his *Elements*. This fact gives us a clue that, sometimes, ignoring rigor and depending on our intuition can also push the work forward.

> If the crystallized deductive form is the goal, intuition and construction are at least the driving forces.

## Chapter I. The Natural Numbers

### Introduction

> "God created the natural numbers; everything else is man's handiwork." In these words Leopold Kronecker (1823–1891) pointed out the safe ground on which the structure of mathematics can be built.

In his book, [*Analysis*](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-981-19-7261-4), Terence Tao even made the natural numbers as handiwork.

#### 3. Computation in Systems Other than the Decimal

> Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716), one of the greatest intellects of his time, was fond of the dyadic system. To quote Laplace: "Leibniz saw in his binary arithmetic the image of creation. He imagined that Unity represented God, and zero the void; that the Supreme Being drew all beings from the void, just as unity and zero express all numbers in his system of numeration."

This may not be just an imagination. Reality may be information encoded in 0s and 1s.
